Fluidity represents the capacity to flow—a property found in gases and liquids that allows them to change form in response to their environment. Some fascinating substances exhibit complex behaviors that blend properties of both solids and liquids. Consider non-Newtonian fluids like a mixture of [water with corn starch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Newtonian_fluid#Oobleck), which demonstrates remarkable properties—becoming more solid-like when subjected to sudden force, then returning to liquid-like behavior when at rest. Conversely, substances like ketchup remain solid until disturbed, after which they flow freely—explaining why a gentle tap on an upturned bottle transforms it from stationary to flowing.

Rigidity, by contrast, represents resistance to flow—responding to forces through opposition rather than adaptation. While rigidity creates an impression of stability and strength, it introduces important engineering challenges: when rigid systems fail, they often do so catastrophically. Well-designed structures like bridges, buildings, and infrastructure must balance necessary stability with sufficient flexibility to absorb unexpected stresses. ## System Responses to Stress

In economic and market systems, we occasionally observe rigid structures experiencing dramatic failures when confronted with unexpected events. These situations sometimes necessitate interventions from governmental bodies or central banks to prevent cascading effects that could spread throughout interconnected systems.

Some materials exhibit extreme rigidity to the point of brittleness—glass and certain hard plastics represent common examples that, while incredibly useful, can shatter completely when stressed beyond their tolerance points. Even with various hardening techniques, [all materials have ultimate failure points](https://youtu.be/XBjiEsAyNQs?t=92) when sufficient force is applied. Understanding these limitations allows us to design appropriate applications and handling procedures for such materials.

## The Advantages of Adaptability

Fluidity, in essence, represents the capacity to adapt, overcome challenges, diffuse stress, and conform to changing circumstances. This concept connects to the mathematical principle of [ergodicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ergodicity), which has profound implications for how we understand systems. In simplified terms, ergodic properties allow representative samples to provide meaningful insights about larger wholes—enabling us to learn through limited exposure rather than requiring complete experience.

This sampling principle offers significant advantages in life: we don't need to experience everything exhaustively to gain valuable understanding. For instance, someone doesn't need to train through an entire Olympic cycle to assess their athletic potential—relatively brief engagement with a sport provides sufficient feedback about aptitude and enjoyment.

## Natural Selection in Systems

Systems characterized by excessive rigidity typically experience significant failures when confronted with unexpected events. Natural selection processes often operate through these failures—less adaptable systems collapse under stress, while more fluid and resilient ones survive. This mechanism effectively removes vulnerable rigid structures and favors those with greater adaptive capacity.

A classic illustration of this principle comes from [Abraham Wald's aircraft survivorship analysis during World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Survivorship_bias#Military). Engineers observed returning aircraft and initially considered reinforcing the areas showing damage. Wald's breakthrough insight was recognizing that they should instead reinforce the undamaged areas—since planes with damage to those regions never returned, representing critical vulnerability points.

## The Integration Point Challenge

In hybrid systems combining both rigid and fluid components, failures typically manifest at integration points where these different properties meet—junctions and interfaces that haven't been optimized to accommodate stress transitions. Interestingly, the fluid portions of such systems often help dissipate and absorb stresses, creating higher overall tolerance than purely rigid alternatives.

## Personal Adaptability

On a personal level, individuals with rigid mindsets often struggle with adaptation as circumstances evolve, facing particular challenges during periods of hardship or when expectations aren't met. This rigidity can create dependencies on external intervention rather than developing self-sufficiency and resilience.

By contrast, people who cultivate fluid approaches demonstrate greater capacity to navigate changing circumstances, recognizing impermanence as a fundamental aspect of life. They develop the ability to identify and leverage opportunities within changing environments rather than expending energy resisting inevitable shifts. Those with adaptive mindsets understand that while chance plays a significant role in outcomes, one can strategically position oneself to encounter more opportunities and pursue asymmetric possibilities—where potential benefits substantially outweigh limited downside risks.

The cultivation of personal adaptability represents one of the most valuable capabilities for navigating our increasingly complex and rapidly changing world.

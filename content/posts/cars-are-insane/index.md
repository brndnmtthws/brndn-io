## Transportation in Transition

If you happen to know me personally, you're probably familiar with my perspective on urban transportation, and perhaps even tired of hearing about it. I follow communities like [r/fuckcars](https://www.reddit.com/r/fuckcars/) and enjoy YouTube channels such as "Not Just Bikes" and "Climate Town."

I believe that future generations will likely view the internal combustion engine era of personal transportation as a transitional phase in human development - a stepping stone on our path toward more sustainable, efficient, and community-oriented transportation systems. Despite our remarkable technological advances and collective intelligence, we have an opportunity to reimagine how we build our communities around more sustainable and health-promoting forms of mobility.

## Examining Transportation Needs

Granted, cars have certain advantages from a strictly utilitarian point of view. However, I'm focusing on the lifestyle that the majority of city-dwelling westerners live today which is one where cars are largely unnecessary for daily activities.

Some people will argue that we _need_ cars for convenience during activities like grocery shopping, but many of these arguments don't stand up to scrutiny when we examine alternative systems that work well in other contexts. I'd suggest that many arguments for the "necessity" of cars are familiar talking points that deserve closer examination.

For the most part, I will happily declare here on my personal blog that most urban dwellers can live happily without a personal car, and might even find improvements in their quality of life without them.

## The Cost-Benefit Analysis

To understand why our current transportation model merits reconsideration, we should think about it in terms of costs and benefits. The costs are substantial: environmental impacts, traffic injuries and fatalities, economic burdens of ownership, infrastructure maintenance, and the complex systems required to manage these challenges.

To understand the health implications, let's look at the [leading causes of death in the US](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/fastats/leading-causes-of-death.htm):

## Transportation and Public Health

Many of these health conditions connect to our transportation systems. Heart disease can be mitigated with more physical activity. Cancer rates are influenced by environmental pollutants, to which vehicles contribute significantly. Diabetes prevalence relates to exercise patterns. Respiratory diseases correlate with air quality. Traffic incidents account for a significant portion of accidental deaths (approximately 40,000 annually in the US).

## Environmental and Health Impacts

Exploring the pollution question further, research indicates that [6-7 million deaths annually are associated with pollution](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanplh/article/PIIS2542-5196(22)00090-0/fulltext). While it's complex to isolate transportation-specific pollution (direct emissions, tire particles, brake dust) from industrial sources, evidence suggests it's a significant factor.

In urban environments particularly, vehicular emissions represent a primary pollution source, contributing to cancer, respiratory conditions, and other health challenges.

Transportation infrastructure also affects natural environments. Highways and roads replace vegetation that would otherwise help filter air pollutants. While adding greenery alongside roads can help mitigate pollution, preserving green spaces offers greater benefits.

Research has connected the [obesity epidemic to car-dependent lifestyles](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15261894/). Studies have linked roadway pollution to [neurological conditions including dementia, Parkinson's, Alzheimer's and multiple sclerosis](https://ehjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12940-020-0565-4), as well as [lung cancer](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4448375/), [diabetes](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanpla/article/PIIS2542-5196(18)30140-2/fulltext), and [heart disease](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2844969/). [The EPA provides additional research on these connections](https://www.epa.gov/mobile-source-pollution/research-health-effects-exposure-risk-mobile-source-pollution).

For many years, vehicle crashes were the leading direct cause of death for young Americans, though recently [firearms and opioid overdoses have surpassed them](https://www.cdc.gov/injury/wisqars/animated-leading-causes.html) for [certain demographic groups](https://www.npr.org/2022/04/22/1094364930/firearms-leading-cause-of-death-in-children).

Research has also shown that pollution [affects cognitive function](https://www.pnas.org/doi/full/10.1073/pnas.1809474115), which presents additional societal challenges.

## Reimagining Community Mobility

It invites us to consider: what kind of communities could we create if we prioritized human health and wellbeing in our transportation planning? How might our cities transform if we embraced the full spectrum of mobility options - from walking and cycling to efficient public transit and shared vehicle systems? Even vibrant cities like New York could become safer, cleaner, and more enjoyable with thoughtful transportation evolution.

Rethinking our relationship with transportation offers exciting possibilities for creating healthier, more connected, and more sustainable communities for everyone.
